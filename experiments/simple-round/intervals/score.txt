simple-round candidate: intervals (predictive-interval / uncertainty tuning)
=============================================================================
Fixed point forecast throughout: independent AR(6) per location, OLS on
the fourth-root-transformed, backfill-corrected vintage series -- i.e.
submissions/seabbs_bot-ar6bf/generate_forecasts.jl's point forecast,
completely unchanged. Only the predictive DISTRIBUTION around that
point forecast is varied below. Scored on VALIDATION SEASONS (1, 2)
ONLY, 2596 tasks (59 origins x 11 locations x 4 horizons), against
target-data/oracle-output.csv, natural scale (operational; log1p is
report-only, never used to select).

Baseline to beat: seabbs_bot-ar6bf (Gaussian innovations at raw fitted
resid_sd) mean WIS (natural) = 0.359.

WINNER: Student-t(df=10) innovations, variance-matched to resid_sd,
then scaled by 1.4.
  mean_wis (natural)      = 0.3483   (-3.0% vs 0.359 baseline)
  sd_wis   (natural)      = 0.3971
  mean_wis (log1p, report-only) = 0.101  (vs ar6bf's 0.103 -- improves
                                           on both scales, ranking does
                                           not flip)
  coverage 50% nominal -> 0.525 actual
  coverage 90% nominal -> 0.892 actual
  per-horizon coverage (h=1..4): cov50 in [0.512, 0.553], cov90 in
    [0.884, 0.906] -- flat across horizons, no evidence of needing a
    per-horizon adjustment (see below).
Implemented in generate.jl (T_DF=10, T_SCALE=1.4). Verified end-to-end
by running generate.jl's own main() and rescoring: mean_wis=0.3483,
sd_wis=0.3971, cov50=0.525, cov90=0.892 -- matches the sweep exactly.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------
Summary
-----------------------------------------------------------------------
- Gaussian, Student-t and bootstrap all bottom out in the same
  scale~1.4-1.5 neighbourhood once properly inflated -- the raw fitted
  resid_sd is simply too narrow (50%/90% nominal intervals cover only
  ~41%/78% at scale=1.0), regardless of distributional family.
- Once each family is inflated to its own optimum, Student-t (moderate
  df) wins: same or slightly better mean WIS than Gaussian/bootstrap,
  AND markedly better calibrated (cov50/cov90 near nominal, vs the
  Gaussian/bootstrap optima's cov50 ~0.53-0.57, i.e. visibly
  over-covered). The heavier tail supplies genuine extra tail mass
  where a Gaussian/bootstrap needs blunt over-dispersion instead.
- Per-horizon variance scaling was tested and rejected (null result,
  made WIS worse): the fixed AR(6) recursion already handles
  horizon-growth correctly, this axis had nothing further to give.
- Final choice: Student-t(df=10), variance-matched, scale=1.4.
  mean_wis (natural, validation 1,2) = 0.3483, beating the 0.359
  baseline by ~3.0%, well-calibrated at both the 50% and 90% level.
