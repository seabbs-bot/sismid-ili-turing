Forms tried:
  - Fourier(1): sin/cos at the annual harmonic, OLS alongside the
    AR(6) lags. Helps (0.3214 vs 0.3684) -- fewer harmonics than the
    naive Fourier(3) that hurt (0.412) is enough to avoid overfitting
    the ~2-season AR training window.
  - Fourier(2): two harmonics, OLS. No better than baseline (0.3678)
    -- already too many free seasonal parameters for ~2 seasons of
    data.
  - Fourier(3), ridge-penalised (lambda in {10, 30, 100}, penalty on
    the 6 harmonic coefficients only, AR lags unpenalised): best at
    lambda=30 (0.3521), still worse than Fourier(1) or climatology.
    Ridge tempers the overfit but does not fix the deeper problem --
    three harmonics fit to ~2 seasons chase those seasons' particular
    peak timing, not the recurring annual shape.
  - Climatology: single extra regressor per location/split, a
    smoothed (5-week circular moving average) median-by-week-of-season
    curve built from the FULL historical series
    (data/flu_data_hhs.csv), using only weeks strictly before that
    split's own forecast origin (no test-season or future leakage).
    Enters the AR(6) regression as one more OLS coefficient, free to
    shrink to ~0 itself if uninformative. Best single-term seasonal
    form (0.3061 alone).
  - Climatology + ridge (lambda=5) on that one coefficient: worse
    (0.3564) -- the climatology term is already regularised by its
    construction (robust median + smoothing over the FULL history,
    not the 2-season AR window), so an extra ridge penalty just
    shrinks it back toward the unhelpful baseline.
  - Climatology + Fourier(1) combined: worse than either alone
    (0.3299) -- two competing seasonal representations re-overfit
    the same signal, echoing the original naive-Fourier(3) failure
    mode.
  - Climatology + backfill correction (seabbs_bot-ar6bf's profile):
    best overall, 0.3004, a further improvement over climatology
    alone -- the two corrections are near-orthogonal (backfill fixes
    recent-week vintage noise; climatology adds calendar-shape
    information) and stack cleanly.

CONCLUSION: regularised seasonality DOES help here, clearly and
robustly (0.3004 vs 0.359 AR6+backfill / 0.368 AR6 alone, ~16-19%
mean-WIS improvement, consistent across locations, horizons, and both
validation seasons) -- but only in the "climatology" form (smoothed
circular median from the FULL historical series, entering as a single
per-location regression coefficient), not as free Fourier harmonics
fit to the ~2-season AR window, which either fail to help (Fourier(2))
or actively overfit (naive Fourier(3): 0.412) even after ridge
shrinkage tempers but does not fix them. The regularisation that
matters is in how the seasonal signal is ESTIMATED (borrow strength
across all of history, smooth it, then let a single OLS coefficient
decide how much to use), not merely in penalising free coefficients
after the fact.

ADDENDUM: pooled (cross-location) seasonal shape, added on request
------------------------------------------------------------------
Follow-up ask: instead of a per-location climatology curve (each
location's shape estimated only from its own history), pool ONE
shared week-of-season shape across all 11 locations + history, with a
small per-location amplitude scaling -- the more principled way to
add seasonality without overfitting the ~2-season training window,
since a shared shape borrows strength across locations as well as
across historical seasons.

Implementation: for each location, first remove that location's own
level (subtract its overall median wILI on the fourthroot scale) so
absolute-scale differences across locations (e.g. Region 6 ~8x Region
8's mean wILI, docs/eda/03-seasonality.md) don't bias the pooled
shape, then bin/smooth that deviation curve by week-of-season exactly
as the per-location climatology (5-week circular moving average of
the circular-bin median). Average the 11 locations' smoothed
deviation curves into ONE shared curve per split (still capped at
that split's own forecast origin, no leakage). Each location then
gets its own free OLS amplitude coefficient on this one shared curve,
fit alongside the AR(6) lags exactly like the per-location
climatology's own coefficient -- so the "small per-location amplitude
scaling" falls out of the same regression, no extra machinery.

Result (same validation-only sweep):
  pooled-clim               0.3090   sd 0.3976
  pooled-clim + backfill    0.3023   sd 0.3945
  pooled-clim + ridge(5) on the amplitude coefficient: 0.3571 (worse,
    same pattern as the per-location climatology -- it's already
    regularised by construction, extra shrinkage just pulls back
    toward baseline)

CONCLUSION on pooling: YES, the pooled/shared seasonal shape also
clearly beats both baselines (0.359 AR6+backfill, 0.368 AR6 alone),
confirming seasonality helps via either route. It is very close to,
but not quite as good as, the per-location climatology already picked
(0.3023 vs 0.3004 with backfill; 0.3090 vs 0.3061 without) -- with 17
years of location-specific history available (data/flu_data_hhs.csv
starts 2003), there is enough data to estimate each location's own
week-of-season shape directly without needing to borrow across
locations, and per-location peak timing genuinely differs (SD 5.2-7.9
weeks across locations, docs/eda/03-seasonality.md) so a shared shape
necessarily blurs that. The per-location climatology + backfill
(0.3004) remains this family's pick and is what generate.jl
implements; the pooled form is recorded here as a validated, working
alternative (same regularisation principle, marginally more
conservative) if a lower-per-location-overfitting-risk choice is
preferred for the final ensemble/selection.
